Write Up

This exercise involved setting up and calibrating the Duckiebot, as well as running demos to showcase its capabilities. Below are the key points:

  • The purpose of calibrating the camera and motors is to ensure accurate perception and control of the Duckiebot.
  • Docker is useful as it provides an isolated and consistent environment for running the Duckiebot software. We also ran into issues due to newer versions of Ubuntu, which required creating a virtual environment to download all the necessary dependencies.
  • Drift during straight-line driving was often caused by wheels that were misshaped or uneven, affecting the accuracy of the bot’s movement.
  • In the lane-following demo, the Duckiebot initially struggled at the first corner. We theorized that this was caused by network delays due to too many people using the same network. This was further supported by the bot making a huge deviation to get back on track, suggesting a delay in communication between the computer and the robot. However, as the demo went on, the Duckiebot followed the lane quite well.
  • Another challenge occurred when we were packing up. Our robot wouldn’t turn off, despite trying various solutions. Not even Adam, who had more experience, could resolve it. He said he would manually unplug the robot safely when we left.
  • The Duckiebot likely uses a combination of computer vision and control algorithms to follow the lane, balancing input from the camera and motor commands.

Key takeaways: Robots are not always perfect. While calibrating them is important, there will always be external factors that affect their performance, such as uneven mats on the road caused by accidental steps. Calibration could also be flawed, as seen with one side of the calibration box where the tape was coming off, causing the images not to be flat. Robots require a lot of work, and instead of always blaming the code when something doesn't work, I now recognize the importance of considering potential hardware issues with the robot.
